A Vacant House & a Decade of Code Cases

Nine Years on Creekbluff

A house gutted to the studs and left unoccupied since 2016 has drawn code complaints for nearly a decade. The public record shows enforcement stalling and closing again and again — while the deed kept moving among the same hands, and finally into a company owned by the property's two prior owners.

By Mark Garcia, President, Bull Creek Home Owners Association
Grounded in City of Austin code records, Notices of Violation, and Texas public filings
Published July 11, 2026

At 7609 Creekbluff Dr, on the edge of the Bull Creek greenbelt in Austin's District 10, sits a single-family house that no one has lived in for the better part of a decade. Its interior was taken down to the studs. Its walls have stood open, its wiring exposed, its yard used at times to store pallets of construction stone and a shipping container. Neighbors have watched it since at least 2017. The City of Austin has opened code case after code case. And yet, nine years on, the house is still gutted, still empty, and still — by the community's account — a hazard on the creek.

This report assembles what the public record actually shows: the 311 service requests, the code case histories, the Notices of Violation, an administrative citation, an "express permit," the chain of title, and the Texas Secretary of State filing for the company that now owns the house. It is written by the president of the neighborhood's homeowners association, who has been the lead correspondent with the City on this property since 2019. Where the record establishes a fact, it is cited to the document. Where the record raises a question it does not answer, that question is framed as a question — not an accusation.

Two findings sit at the center of the story. First, the City's own enforcement history is one of repeated near-misses: violations found, notices sent, deadlines set — and then cases that stall for years awaiting an administrative hearing that never gets scheduled, or close on "voluntary compliance" that the neighborhood disputes. Second, and newly documented, the property's ownership has shuffled among a small, connected set of people; in July 2025 the two individuals who owned the house across the prior decade — Stephen W. Johnson and Michael "Mike" Bueker — formed a single limited liability company together and moved the deed into it.1

I. The Property, and the Pattern

7609 Creekbluff Dr is legally described as Lot 18, Block C, Lakewood Park Section 4, and carries Travis Central Appraisal District parcel number 0147090104. It is zoned SF‑2 — standard single-family residential.2 That zoning matters, because for years the neighborhood watched the lot used as something else: a place to park a contractor's materials.

The house has been the subject of City of Austin code complaints continuously since 2017, under a series of case numbers that trace the same underlying condition — a residence gutted by unpermitted work and then abandoned in that state. The through-line across every one of those cases is not just the address. It is the cast of people attached to it, and the way the title has moved among them each time enforcement drew close.

Confirmed — Chain of title & Texas SOS filing

The deed to 7609 Creekbluff Dr passed from Gilyan & Johnson Investments LLC (2016) to Stephen W. & Angela D. Johnson (2018) to Michael Jeffrey Bueker (2022) and, on July 30, 2025, to 7609 Creekbluff LLC (instrument 2025084464). That LLC (Texas SOS filing #806107975) was formed just 28 days earlier, on July 2, 2025, and lists Steve W. Johnson and Mike Bueker as its two Managing Members — at the same mailing address.1

In plain terms: the man who owned the house from 2018 to 2022 (Johnson) and the man who bought it from him in 2022 (Bueker) are, as of 2025, business partners in the company that now holds the title. The property's ownership changed on paper four times in nine years. The people behind it did not.

The address stayed the same. The names on the deed changed. The house never did.

II. 2017–2018 — Gutted, No Permits, a First Citation

The oldest complaints in the file date to the spring and fall of 2017. On October 3, 2017, a complaint reached the Austin Code Department describing a home that had "been under construction" for the past two years with "no building permit posted." Inspector Ronald Union visited on October 16, 2017 and documented what he found from the public right of way.3

Confirmed — Case history CC‑2017‑125062, inspector's note (10/16/2017)

"I observed a vacant structure with extensive construction work already performed. I observed all the interior walls and ceiling area had been opened up (all the sheet rock had been removed), also there were exposed electrical wires throughout the structure… I did not observe any active construction permits for this location." The inspector spoke with owner Steve Johnson (512‑845‑5026), who "admitted to removing the sheetrock" and said he was "in the permit process."3

What followed set the template for everything after. A contractor's sign — "Upper Construction, 512‑730‑0777" — stood in the front yard. Inspectors posted Notices of Violation in February 2018. And over and over, when reached by phone, the owner said the plans were nearly ready:

  • Feb 17, 2018 Owner "said he was working on the plan review as we were speaking… He said he plans to have the plans submitted by this Friday."3
  • Apr 27, 2018 Owner "stated that the new architects are submitting the plan documentation on Monday."3
  • Oct 24, 2018 Owner "stated that he has obtained his engineer drawings and would be starting his permit process tomorrow."3

None of those submissions materialized. By the City's own record, the plan review for the property had expired and no active permits existed. On July 5, 2018, the City posted Administrative Citation 002474 for work without a permit, and the case was flagged for an administrative hearing.4 Then it stalled. Month after month through 2019, the inspector's follow-ups read the same way: "No change has occurred at this location and an administrative hearing date is in the process of being assigned."3

Open question

An administrative citation was posted in July 2018 and the case was repeatedly described as "awaiting an administrative hearing date" through late 2019. The records in hand do not show that a contested administrative hearing on the 2017–2018 violations was ever actually held before the case was reworked and ultimately closed. Whether — and when — a hearing occurred is a question for the City.

III. 2019 — The Neighborhood Escalates

By late 2019 the condition of the property had become a standing item for the Bull Creek HOA. What had tipped neighbors over the edge, as the association's November 2019 correspondence put it, was the appearance of "8–10 pallets of stone rock… in the middle of the yard" that "remained there for the last 6 months," alongside a 20‑by‑40 storage container — the lot being used, in effect, as a contractor's yard in a residential zone.5

On November 2, 2019, the HOA filed three separate code service requests and wrote to the District 10 council office, then held by Council Member Alison Alter, laying out the neighborhood's concerns: a gutted, vacant house directly on the greenbelt as a fire risk; an attractive nuisance to children; and a magnet for squatters.5

Confirmed — 311 service requests & District 10 response, Nov 2019

The HOA filed service requests 19‑00422038, 19‑00422049, and 19‑00422056 (contractor's-yard use, unoccupied structure, and the storage container). District 10 constituent liaison Mina Shekarchi confirmed the office would open a Citizen Assistance Form (CAF), which "escalates your concerns to the relevant members of City staff and requires they respond to you within 10 days."5

The escalation produced movement. A newly assigned investigator, Khalid Marshall, wrote to the HOA on November 26, 2019 that he had "recently inherited this case" and could see "that this case may have been mishandled in the past."6 On November 6, 2019 the City issued a Notice of Violation citing three items: graffiti on the storage container (§302.9), residential construction performed without permits with sheetrock removed and wiring exposed (§25‑12‑241), and the storage of construction stone on a residential lot (§10‑5‑21).7

IV. 2019–2020 — The "Express Permit," and How the Case Closed

What happened next is the first instance of a pattern the neighborhood would come to distrust: a case that looked like it was heading to legal enforcement instead resolved through a narrow permit and quiet closure.

In December 2019, an express permit appeared on the property's record. Per the City's case history, plan review PR 2019‑234798 was created on December 11, 2019 for "Express: remove sheetrock and insulation for exploratory purposes," followed by building permit BP 2019‑237817.3 The HOA questioned how an express permit — a fast-track path meant for limited, low-risk work — could apply to a house where, in the association's recollection, electrical, floor, wall, and plumbing work had already been done.8 Through early 2020, inspectors chased the removal of the storage container and trailer, and the case moved toward closure.

Unresolved — the express-permit closure

The neighborhood's contention is that a house gutted by extensive interior work was allowed to resolve its "work without permit" violation through an exploratory express permit, rather than the full permitting the prior demolition would ordinarily require. Whether the express permit properly covered the work already performed is a question the City's permitting record should be able to answer.

V. 2022–2023 — A New Name on the Deed, the Same House

On October 14, 2022, the property changed hands again: Stephen and Angela Johnson deeded it to Michael Jeffrey Bueker.1 The HOA noted the sale in the spring of 2023 and understood the buyer to be a real-estate developer.9 Through the association's architectural committee, there were intermittent assurances that cleanup and construction were about to begin — the same language the neighborhood had heard for years.9 The house stayed as it was.

In August 2023, the HOA filed a fresh, formal complaint — this time framed explicitly under the International Property Maintenance Code that Austin has adopted, asking the City to evaluate the structure as unsafe and unfit for human occupancy, an attractive nuisance, and a hazard on the greenbelt.10 The complaint generated a code case that, months later, was closed as "Voluntary Compliance."

The neighborhood's central grievance

According to the HOA, the 2023 code violation was closed on the basis that permits had been "verified as filed" — yet a search of the City's public permit records showed zero permits on file for the property. Closing an enforcement case on a remediation that the public record does not appear to support is the specific failure the association raised, in writing, to the City. The underlying closure notes are the subject of a public-information request.

VI. 2024 — A Formal Escalation to the Council

On January 14, 2024, the HOA president wrote directly to Council Member Alison Alter and her staff, describing a problem "we have had in our neighborhood for over 5 years," concerning a property "not a primary residence for the owner" that had "been gutted and not occupied for the last 7 years."11 The letter set out two challenges in the plainest terms the association could: first, the closing of a code case "with false remediation information"; second, the City's failure to inspect and assess the full set of unsafe-structure violations the neighborhood had cited.11

District 10 relayed the letter to Development Services, which sent an inspector back out. The City's February 2024 response reported the home "well maintained from the outside," with an enclosed trailer, "some old construction material behind the trailer hidden from public view," two deteriorating wood columns, a small section of broken fascia, and a construction sign near the garage — and said a new notice would be sent "for the deficiencies and working outside the scope of the permit."12

VII. 2025 — The Ownership Shuffle

This is the development that reframes the entire history. In the summer of 2025, the two men who had owned 7609 Creekbluff across the prior decade formalized a partnership around it.

  • July 2, 2025 7609 Creekbluff LLC is formed with the Texas Secretary of State (filing #806107975). Its two Managing Members are Steve W. Johnson and Mike Bueker, both listed at 3100 Kramer Ln Apt 534, Austin, TX 78758.1
  • July 30, 2025 Michael Bueker deeds 7609 Creekbluff Dr to 7609 Creekbluff LLC (warranty deed, instrument 2025084464) — 28 days after the company that receives it was created.1

The Secretary of State record is unambiguous on the point that ties the decade together: the property's 2018–2022 owner of record and its 2022–2025 owner of record are the same two people who now jointly control the LLC holding title. The house's ownership has, in substance, never left them.1

What the record shows — and what it does not

Documented: the deed chain and the SOS filing showing Johnson and Bueker as co-managers of the entity that now owns the property, formed weeks before the transfer.1 Not established by these documents: the owners' intent in restructuring ownership. The HOA's concern — that transferring a property among related owners and entities can interrupt or reset the momentum of a code case headed toward an administrative hearing — is a pattern the timeline supports, but intent is a matter for the City and, if it comes to it, a tribunal to weigh.

VIII. 2026 — A New Council, a New Notice

District 10 changed hands, and the neighborhood tried again. In late May 2026, Council Member Marc Duchen attended the Bull Creek HOA's annual meeting and Memorial Day barbecue. Afterward, the council member and his communications director, Anne‑Charlotte Patterson, walked the Creekbluff property and photographed it. On June 2, 2026, Patterson wrote that the office's land-use policy strategist would be reaching out about "the vacant home on Creekbluff," and that Council Member Duchen "will stay engaged with this as well."13

The same day, the District 10 office relayed the Code Compliance office's own update. It made a distinction the neighborhood has long run up against — and then confirmed that a fresh notice had gone out:

Confirmed — District 10 office, relaying Code Compliance (June 2, 2026)

"The fact that the property is unoccupied long-term is not a code violation in of itself… However, Code Compliance did conduct an inspection… A Notice of Violations has been sent to the property owner, and we will continue to observe… If we do not have voluntary compliance within the allotted compliance timeframe, the case will be transitioned to legal escalation."14

That notice is Notice of Violation CV‑2026‑067670, dated June 2, 2026, issued by Austin Code Inspector Erica Thompson and addressed to 7609 Creekbluff LLC, PO Box 684196, Austin. It cites structure-maintenance violations — front porch posts missing protective treatment and footings (§304.2), and missing siding and brick facade on the left side and front-left corner of the house (§304.6) — plus grass over 12 inches (§10‑5‑21), the same abatement provision from 2019.15

The 2026 notice also contains a clause that speaks directly to the ownership history. It states that after receipt of the notice, and until compliance is attained, the City Code prohibits the sale, lease, or transfer of the property unless the owner provides any buyer or transferee a copy of the notice and gives the City the transferee's name and address.15 After years in which the deed moved each time enforcement drew near, the current notice attaches a transfer restriction to the case.

IX. Where Things Stand

As of July 2026, the house at 7609 Creekbluff Dr remains gutted and unoccupied. An open Notice of Violation (CV‑2026‑067670) is in its compliance window, with the District 10 office stating that failure to comply will move the case to "legal escalation." Council Member Duchen's office has said it will stay engaged. The neighborhood has been here before — an open case, a promise of follow-through — several times over nine years.

Established by the record

  • Since 2017 The house has been gutted to the studs, unpermitted, and unoccupied, with continuous code complaints.3
  • 2018 An administrative citation (002474) was posted; the case then sat "awaiting an administrative hearing" through 2019.4
  • 2025 Title moved into an LLC co-managed by the property's two prior owners of record, formed 28 days before the transfer.1
  • 2026 A new Notice of Violation is open against that LLC, carrying a sale/transfer restriction.15

Open questions for the City

  • Enforcement Was a contested administrative hearing on the 2017–2018 violations ever held, and if not, why did the citation lapse?
  • Closure integrity On what basis was the 2023 code case closed as "voluntary compliance," given that public permit records showed no permits on file?
  • Unsafe structure Has any inspection formally evaluated the structure against the unsafe-/vacant-structure and attractive-nuisance provisions the HOA cited (IPMC §§111, 202.2, 305.1.1), rather than only exterior-maintenance items?

What the neighborhood is asking

The Bull Creek HOA is asking the City to treat 7609 Creekbluff not as a dormant construction project but as what the record describes: a long-abandoned, gutted structure on the greenbelt. Concretely, that means a full unsafe-structure assessment, follow-through on the open 2026 notice to a genuine administrative hearing if compliance is not met, and a review of how prior cases on this address were closed.

Methodology & Sourcing

This report is built from primary records: City of Austin code case histories and Notices of Violation obtained through public-information requests; 311 service-request confirmations; recorded deeds searchable through the Travis County Clerk; the Travis Central Appraisal District parcel record; and the Texas Secretary of State business-entity filing for 7609 Creekbluff LLC. Direct quotations are transcribed from those documents.

Correspondence between the HOA, neighbors, and City officials is quoted where it establishes an official action or response; private individuals' personal contact information has been omitted. Statements of the neighborhood's belief or contention are labeled as such and separated from documented facts. Where the record raises a question it does not resolve, the report frames it as an open question rather than a conclusion.
