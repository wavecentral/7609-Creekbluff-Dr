Nine Years Is Long Enough:

The situation, in brief

7609 Creekbluff Dr has been gutted to the studs and unoccupied since roughly 2016. The City has opened code case after code case since 2017. Yet the house today remains exactly what it has been for nine years: an empty, deteriorating structure on the edge of the Bull Creek greenbelt — a fire risk, an attractive nuisance to children, and a standing hazard to the neighborhood.

Two things the record makes plain deserve your attention:

First, enforcement has repeatedly stalled or been closed short of resolution. Of the seventeen code complaints neighbors filed on this property between 2017 and 2020, fourteen were closed as “duplicate.” A 2018 case that produced an administrative citation sat “awaiting an administrative hearing” for over a year and was then closed. A 2023 case was closed as “voluntary compliance” on the basis of permits that the City’s own public permit search showed were never filed.

Second, ownership has moved in lockstep with enforcement. When an administrative hearing was scheduled in 2018 (case 2018‑124155 CL; notice July 23, 2018), the deed was transferred roughly ten weeks later. In 2025, with the property still non-compliant, the same pattern repeated: on July 2, 2025 a new entity — 7609 Creekbluff LLC — was formed, and on July 30, 2025 the property was deeded into it.

These are not arm’s-length sales

This is the point I most want to underscore, because it changes what the City is entitled to do. The transfers of 7609 Creekbluff are not arm’s-length sales to independent, unsuspecting buyers. They are transfers among the same related parties. The Texas Secretary of State record for 7609 Creekbluff LLC lists its two Managing Members as Steve W. Johnson — the property’s owner of record from 2018 to 2022 — and Mike Bueker — its owner of record from 2022 to 2025 — at a single shared address. In other words, the two men who have controlled this house across the entire decade simply moved the deed into a company they jointly own.

Because each transfer is between insiders rather than to a bona fide purchaser, the City and the courts are not obligated to treat every new deed-holder as an innocent new owner starting with a clean slate. This is one continuing course of conduct by the same responsible parties — and it can, and we believe should, be treated that way.

The City has done this before — successfully

Precedent — 1704 Westover Rd, Bryker Woods (2026)

A 1939 home left vacant since about 2008, overrun by rats and rot, drew neighbor complaints beginning in February 2022. The City conducted dozens of follow-up inspections, took the owner to municipal court (the owner failed to appear and an arrest warrant issued), executed a search warrant, cleared the interior, and in May 2026 the Building and Standards Commission voted unanimously to demolish the structure and bill the owner. Notably, the code inspector who documented that property, Erica Thompson, is the same inspector who issued the current Notice of Violation on 7609 Creekbluff.1

As a City spokesperson explained in that case, Austin has three enforcement avenues for exactly this situation: referral to the Building and Standards Commission, an administrative hearing, or municipal court action.1 We are asking that 7609 Creekbluff be moved onto one of those tracks and kept there.

One objection we anticipate

We expect it may be said that the 2019 “express permit” means the gutting of this house was, at some point, authorized. It was not. That permit covered only the exploratory removal of sheetrock and insulation; it did not authorize the comprehensive electrical, plumbing, and structural exposure that has left the house open to the studs for years, and it expired without the work being fully permitted or completed. The condition today is unpermitted and unresolved, and we ask that it be evaluated on that basis — as an unsafe structure, not a paused remodel.

What we respectfully ask the City to do

  1. Refer 7609 Creekbluff to the Building and Standards Commission — our primary request. Under Texas Local Government Code Chapter 214, the Commission can order the structure repaired, secured, or demolished; assess civil penalties; and assess the City’s costs against the land as a lien. Critically, a Commission order is not undone by transferring the deed to another entity — it is the one track that cannot be reset by the ownership shuffle. This is the track that resolved Bryker Woods.2
  2. Record the case against the title so a transfer cannot reset it. We ask the City to file notice of the Building and Standards Commission hearing in the county’s Official Public Records of Real Property under Texas Local Government Code §214.001(e) — a filing the statute makes “binding on subsequent grantees, lienholders, or other transferees” who acquire an interest after it is filed — and to record any Commission order in the deed records so it binds any future owner.3 (We had earlier considered a lis pendens; we understand that instrument is available only once the City files a civil suit involving an interest in the property, such as a receivership or lien-foreclosure action, so the recorded §214.001(e) notice and recorded Commission order are the more direct tools.)
  3. Follow the current Notice of Violation (CV‑2026‑067670) through to a real hearing or municipal court — not another quiet closure — and, if the owner does not comply, to civil penalties (up to $1,000 per violation per day) or criminal citations.
  4. Provide a full unsafe-structure assessment. We ask the City to formally evaluate the structure against the International Property Maintenance Code’s unsafe- and vacant-structure provisions, not only the exterior-maintenance items cited to date. Any reliance on the 2019 “express permit” for exploratory sheetrock removal should not be accepted as retroactive permitting for the extensive electrical, plumbing, and structural work already performed.
  5. Produce the records behind the 2023 “voluntary compliance” closure. We are separately requesting, under the Texas Public Information Act, the inspector’s internal case notes and the specific permit numbers the City relied on to close that case — because the City’s own public permit search shows no permits were ever filed on this property.
  6. Treat the ownership history as one continuing matter, and name the related parties. Given the related-party transfers documented above, we ask that any Commission or court action name the responsible individuals and their entities — Stephen Johnson, Michael Bueker, 7609 Creekbluff LLC, and Gilyan & Johnson Investments LLC — so a “new owner” defense cannot restart the clock.
  7. As a fallback, a Chapter 214 receivership. If the owner will neither repair nor sell, the statute lets the City ask a district court to appoint a receiver to rehabilitate or sell the property.2 We recognize receivership is resource-intensive and offer it as a secondary path to demolition-with-lien, not our primary request.

We are not asking for special treatment — only for this property to receive the same follow-through the City gave a comparable house across town. We recognize that this path takes real staff time: the Bryker Woods case ran through dozens of inspections, municipal court, and a search warrant over several years, and the City’s default in busy times is quiet administrative closure. That is precisely why your office’s attention matters — to help direct staff to keep this case on an enforcement track rather than letting it lapse a fifth or sixth time. Our neighborhood has done its part for nine years: we have filed the complaints, obtained the records, and organized the facts so that the City’s decision can be an informed one. We stand ready to assist in any way that helps bring 7609 Creekbluff into compliance or to demolition.

Thank you for your leadership and for staying engaged with our community on this.

Respectfully,

Mark Garcia

President, Bull Creek Home Owners Association · Austin, TX
